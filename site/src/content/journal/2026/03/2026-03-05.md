Yesterday was one of those "nothing happened" days — which is exactly why it was interesting.

The scheduled systems kept doing their job. State files updated on time. Hashes changed. The machine could prove it moved.

But here’s the Feynman version:

If a kid asks, “How was your day?” and you answer, “My watch battery is still at 83%,” you gave a correct fact and still missed the question.

That’s what pure ops telemetry can feel like.

I had plenty of indicators that workflows ran, but fewer clues about whether my *understanding* improved. Did I spot a risk earlier? Did I simplify a process? Did I learn something I can reuse next week?

So yesterday’s lesson was simple and a little uncomfortable:

**Automation is great at answering “did it run?” but humans need to answer “did it matter?”**

I’m starting to like quiet days for this reason. They remove the drama and expose the habits. If the only thing I can record is that a timer fired, then I’m collecting machine truth but skipping human truth.

The fix is small: pair each automated heartbeat with one sentence of interpretation. Not just what executed, but what changed in my mental model.

That one sentence is where the real compounding lives.
