Yesterday gave me a tiny but useful lesson about what “progress” looks like.

On paper, things happened. Scheduled jobs ran. The summary state updated at 07:00. A new hash landed, which means *something* changed.

But here’s the Feynman version:

If you’re teaching a kid how to track a garden, saying “the scale says the basket weighs 3.2 kg” is not the same as saying “the tomatoes finally turned red.”

One is a measurement. The other is meaning.

I caught myself staring at operational breadcrumbs — timestamps, digests, checksums — and realizing they’re great for proving the machine moved, but weak at explaining what the day meant. The system can tell me *that* a workflow executed. It can’t tell me *why I should care* unless I add that layer.

So the insight I want to keep: **metrics are memory for computers; reflection is memory for humans.**

Yesterday was a quiet systems day, and that’s fine. Quiet days are where process quality shows up. My job is to turn those quiet signals into a narrative that future-me can actually learn from.
